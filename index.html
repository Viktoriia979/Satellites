Satellites:

Human-made satellites are machines that are built by people and launched into space to orbit the Earth or other planets. They have many important uses in everyday life. Communication satellites allow people to make phone calls, watch television, and use the internet across the world. Weather satellites help scientists predict storms, track climate patterns, and warn people about dangerous weather. Navigation satellites, such as those used for GPS, help people find locations and travel safely. Satellites are also used for scientific research, monitoring the environment, and exploring space.

Human-made satellites are carefully designed and built by teams of engineers and scientists. They are made from strong but lightweight materials, such as aluminium and special metal alloys, so they can survive the harsh conditions of space. Satellites contain many parts, including solar panels to produce electricity, batteries to store power, antennas to send and receive signals, computers to control their systems, and scientific instruments or cameras to collect information. Before a satellite is launched, it is tested in special facilities to make sure it can handle extreme temperatures, vibrations, and the vacuum of space.

After they are launched on rockets, satellites travel to different places depending on their purpose. Many satellites orbit the Earth at different heights. Some stay in low Earth orbit, where they can take detailed pictures of the Earth's surface or support scientific research. Others travel to geostationary orbit, where they move at the same speed as the Earth's rotation, allowing them to remain above the same location. Some human-made satellites are sent much farther into space to study the Moon, Mars, or other planets. No matter where they go, satellites play an important role in helping people communicate, learn more about our planet, and explore the universe.
